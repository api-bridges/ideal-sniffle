JEKYLL ISLAND: THE BIRTH OF THE CONTROL MACHINE

In November 1910, seven men representing roughly one-quarter of the entire world's wealth met in total secrecy on Jekyll Island, Georgia, and designed the Federal Reserve System. They travelled in private rail cars with drawn curtains, used only first names, and their meeting was not publicly acknowledged for two decades. The Federal Reserve Act was passed in December 1913, on the last day before Congress recessed for Christmas, when most members had already left Washington.

The system they created was elegant in its brutality: it allowed private banks to create money through lending, charge interest on that newly-created money, and collect collateral in real assets when borrowers defaulted. The money they lent did not previously exist. The collateral they collected — land, homes, businesses — was entirely real. This asymmetry is the foundation of every financial system operating on earth today.

"I am a most unhappy man. I have unwittingly ruined my country. A great industrial nation is now controlled by its system of credit."

— President Woodrow Wilson, 1919, on signing the Federal Reserve Act into law

CREDIT SCORES: COMPLIANCE METRICS IN DISGUISE

The credit score system was introduced as a tool to assess lending risk. What it has become is a behavioral management system. Your score drops if you use too much of your available credit (penalizing economic hardship), drops if you open too many accounts (penalizing shopping for better rates), and drops if you have no credit history (penalizing those who choose not to participate). The system is structured to reward exactly one behavior: continuous debt servicing. Not financial health. Not savings. Not economic independence. Only compliance with debt.
