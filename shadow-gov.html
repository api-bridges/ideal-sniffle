THE PERMANENT GOVERNMENT

The United States federal government employs 2.87 million civilian workers. Of those, fewer than 4,000 are political appointees who change with administrations. The remaining 2.866 million are permanent. The 17 intelligence agencies together employ a further 100,000 — also permanent. These career officials have institutional memory spanning decades. They control document classification, meaning they determine what elected officials can legally know. They control information flow to the President through the Daily Brief — a curated selection that shapes decisions at the highest level.

Every US President since Eisenhower has complained about CIA insubordination. Kennedy threatened to "splinter the CIA into a thousand pieces." Nixon's downfall was engineered in part by FBI and CIA officials who considered him ungovernable. Trump's first term was defined by open conflict with the intelligence community. None of them succeeded in fundamentally restructuring these agencies. The institutions outlasted them all. This is not dysfunction — it is a feature of institutional design.

Decades of media consolidation have intensified every filter. The digital era added a sixth: algorithmic amplification of emotionally resonant content that deepens tribal identity and makes coherent political analysis neurologically unrewarding. The system does not need censors. It has engagement metrics.

Political parties in Western democracies have converged on the same economic framework: central bank independence, free capital movement, and corporate tax competition. The range of policies available through democratic participation has been systematically narrowed by international treaty obligations — WTO, IMF conditionality, EU fiscal rules — that no electorate voted for and no election can reverse. The Overton Window is not a natural phenomenon. It has gardeners.
